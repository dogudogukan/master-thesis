# Bank Subsidiary and FinTech Arm Verification Note

**Date:** February 2026
**Dataset:** `unique_assignees_bank_mapping.csv`

## Objective
This note documents the checks used to assess whether major U.S. banks, or
other large banking groups relevant to the sample, appear in the extracted
assignee universe under unmapped (`is_bank=0`) subsidiary, fintech, innovation-
lab, or holding-company names.

## Review Procedure

1. **Specific subsidiary and fintech-arm scan**  
   Regex searches were run for known technology and innovation entities linked
   to major banks.

   Keywords checked included: Onyx (JPMorgan), Quorum (JPMorgan), WePay
   (JPMorgan), Marcus (Goldman Sachs), numo (PNC), Layer 6 (TD Bank),
   Charles River (State Street), Pershing (BNY Mellon), Elavon (U.S. Bank),
   Paribus (Capital One), GreenSky (Goldman Sachs), Zelle / Early Warning
   Services, and The Clearing House.

   **Result:** No unmapped assignee in the review set matched these entities in
   a way that suggested a missed bank attribution.

2. **Expanded keyword scan**  
   Unmapped assignees were cross-checked against combinations of top-50 bank
   names and terms such as `labs`, `innovations`, `technologies`, `ventures`,
   `capital`, and `digital`.

   **Result:** The matches identified in this scan were limited to independent
   payment networks and market-infrastructure entities such as Mastercard, Visa,
   and DTCC. These were not treated as individual banks for the purposes of the
   current sample.

3. **Manual review of higher-volume unmapped assignees**  
   Around 150 assignees with `is_bank=0` and at least three patents were
   manually reviewed. Ambiguous names containing terms such as "Financial" or
   "Holdings" (for example, *Dearborn Financial Inc.* and *Atrium Separate IP
   Holdings*) were checked individually through web search and firm-level
   verification.

   **Result:** None of the reviewed entities could be linked with sufficient
   confidence to a focal bank group. In the cases examined, the unmapped
   assignees appeared to be independent technology firms, patent-holding
   entities, or non-bank financial infrastructure providers.

## Interpretation
The review does not prove that every possible subsidiary case has been ruled
out. It does, however, suggest that the risk of materially understating bank
patenting through missed fintech arms or hidden subsidiary names is limited in
the current extracted assignee universe.

This pattern is also consistent with broader expectations from the literature.
Patents can serve as signals of innovative capacity under conditions of
information asymmetry (Spence, 1973; Long, 2002). For large banking groups,
there are also governance, disclosure, and portfolio-management reasons to keep
strategically important intellectual property under clearly attributable
entities rather than scattering it across opaque structures. In practice, this
does not imply that all bank-related innovation must appear under the parent
company name. It does suggest, however, that major omissions should be rare
enough to be detectable in the kinds of scans and manual checks documented
above.

## Conclusion
Taken together, the checks reported here support the current
`is_bank=1` mapping as a reasonable basis for the final bank sample. Within the
reviewed assignee universe, no omitted subsidiary, fintech arm, or holding
entity was identified that would materially change the top-50-bank patent
counts. This conclusion should still be read as a validation result for the
current dataset rather than as a universal claim about how banks always assign
their patents.
